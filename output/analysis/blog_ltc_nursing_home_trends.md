# Medicaid's $191 Billion Blind Spot

*The fastest-growing long-term care services are the hardest to audit*

---

![Monthly Medicaid Provider Spending, Jan 2018 – Dec 2024](../charts/monthly_spending_trend.png)

Medicaid spent $191.2 billion on long-term care between January 2018 and December 2024. That is 17.5% of every Medicaid dollar — one in five.

Annual spending more than doubled: $15.5 billion in 2018 to $35.2 billion in 2023. That is 18% annual growth — more than double the 7-8% Medicaid average.

Where is the money going? Away from nursing homes and into people's homes. And that is where the fraud problem starts.

## The shift

In 2018, nursing homes received 13.8% of long-term care dollars. By 2024: 8.3%. Active nursing home providers peaked at 5,102 in 2021, then dropped 25% to 3,843 in three years. The remaining facilities serve sicker patients at higher costs — cost per claim rose 32%, from $81 to $106.

Home-based care absorbed the difference. Home health agencies and in-home personal care now take 84% of long-term care spending, up from 77% in 2018.

| Year | Facility (NH/NF/SNF) | Home-Based (HHA + In-Home) | Assisted Living | Hospice |
|------|----------------------|---------------------------|-----------------|---------|
| 2018 | **13.8%** | **77.3%** | 3.4% | 5.5% |
| 2020 | 10.7% | 81.6% | 3.3% | 4.4% |
| 2023 | 9.8% | 82.5% | 4.0% | 3.7% |
| 2024 | **8.3%** | **83.9%** | 4.2% | 3.5% |

This reflects national policy. States moved patients out of institutions and into home settings. Most people prefer home. Home care costs less per person.

But home care is structurally harder to verify. A nursing home has walls, staff schedules, sign-in logs, other patients. A home visit has none of that. The aide shows up — or doesn't — and a claim gets filed either way.

Put more simply: the system chose convenience over accountability.

## Home health spending doubled

Home health agency spending went from $10.3 billion (2018) to $24.2 billion (2023). Providers grew 34%, from 6,230 to 8,063. Cost per patient rose 55%, from $1,130 to $1,748.

Including in-home supportive care, total home-based spending reached $28.9 billion in 2023 — up from $11.9 billion in 2018. At this growth rate, long-term care spending alone would hit $80 billion by 2028.

| Year | Home Health + In-Home Total | Year-over-Year Growth | Active Providers |
|------|---------------------------|----------------------|-----------------|
| 2018 | $11.9B | — | 7,775 |
| 2019 | $16.5B | +38.3% | 8,339 |
| 2020 | $20.1B | +22.1% | 9,213 |
| 2021 | $23.1B | +14.9% | 9,655 |
| 2022 | $25.2B | +8.8% | 9,848 |
| 2023 | $28.9B | +15.0% | 10,194 |

## One billing code: $122.7 billion

![Top 20 Procedures by Total Spending — T1019 dominates at $122.7B](../charts/top20_procedures.png)

Procedure code T1019 — personal care services, billed in 15-minute increments — accounts for $122.7 billion over the study period. That is 11.2% of all Medicaid spending and more than all facility-based nursing homes combined.

T1019 grew from $9.7 billion (2018) to $22.8 billion (2023). Beneficiaries rose from 5.9 million to 9.2 million. Cost per beneficiary climbed from $1,630 to $2,485.

| Year | T1019 Spending | Beneficiaries | $/Beneficiary | Year-over-Year |
|------|---------------|---------------|---------------|----------------|
| 2018 | $9.7B | 5.9M | $1,630 | — |
| 2019 | $13.8B | 7.4M | $1,874 | +42.3% |
| 2020 | $16.0B | 7.5M | $2,139 | +15.7% |
| 2021 | $17.3B | 8.1M | $2,149 | +8.7% |
| 2022 | $19.6B | 8.5M | $2,317 | +13.3% |
| 2023 | $22.8B | 9.2M | $2,485 | +16.2% |

New York takes 64% of all T1019 dollars nationally. In 2023, $14.7 billion of the $22.8 billion total flowed through New York at $3,375 per beneficiary — double the national average of ~$1,700. This is not random. New York's personal care programs attract fraudulent billing. Our analysis flagged New York home health agencies among the most suspicious providers in the dataset.

## Attendant care quadrupled

Attendant care (code S5125) went from $2.5 billion (2018) to $6.0 billion (2023). Claims volume quadrupled: 21 million to 79 million. Beneficiaries doubled: 1.7 million to 3.5 million.

Services delivered behind closed doors with no independent verification.

## Assisted living: fastest-growing facility type

Assisted living grew from $521 million (2018) to $1.41 billion (2023) — 22% annually, the fastest of any long-term care category. Cost per beneficiary nearly doubled. Providers grew 57%.

Assisted living fills the gap between home care and nursing homes. As nursing homes shrink, assisted living absorbs demand.

## Tennessee's outlier

Tennessee accounts for $5.2 billion — 28% of all nursing home spending nationally. The reason: its Department of Intellectual and Developmental Disabilities bills under "Nursing Home" codes. Two of the top five flagged providers in the entire dataset are Tennessee providers caught in this classification pattern.

## What does this mean?

Three facts:

**The hardest-to-audit services are growing fastest.** Home-based personal care spending is up 135% in five years. Attendant care claims quadrupled. Nursing homes — where services are easier to verify — are shrinking.

**Spending concentrates in ways that amplify risk.** One billing code (T1019) exceeds all nursing homes combined. One state (New York) receives 64% of all personal care dollars.

**Per-person costs are rising across every category.** Nursing homes: +44%. Home health: +55%. Assisted living: +99%. Some of this reflects wage increases and sicker patients. Rising per-person costs also create room for overbilling.

The shift from nursing homes to home care is not the problem. The problem: oversight systems built for institutions were never rebuilt for a home-based world.

At current growth, long-term care spending reaches $80 billion by 2028. That money will flow to services delivered in private homes with no independent verification. The question is whether oversight catches up before the spending does.

---

*Analysis based on CMS Medicaid provider spending data, January 2018 – December 2024. $1.09 trillion in payments across 227 million billing records. 2024 figures may understate actuals by 10-15% due to incomplete year-end submissions.*
